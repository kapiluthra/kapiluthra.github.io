Who I Am

Most enterprises don't have a content problem. They have a findability problem, a governance problem, and an AI-readiness problem — all wearing the same disguise.

  • After 14+ years working inside OpenText Content Server and Extended ECM ecosystems across Europe, the Middle East, and the Americas, I've seen the same pattern repeat: millions invested in ECM platforms, yet knowledge remains trapped in fragmented repositories, disconnected from the systems where decisions actually get made. This is the gap I help enterprises close.
  • I partner with CIOs, Enterprise Architects, and digital transformation leaders to modernise OpenText-based content ecosystems — combining xECM, cloud-native architectures, and AI-driven capabilities to turn static repositories into decision-grade knowledge assets.
  • My role typically sits at the intersection of enterprise architecture, solution strategy, and execution. I help leaders define ECM and AI-readiness roadmaps, reduce integration sprawl, design scalable secure architectures, and tie ECM investments to measurable business outcomes.
  • Early in my career I watched a programme stall — not because the technology failed, but because nobody had solved for adoption, governance, or the human side of content. That lesson shaped how I work ever since. Deep platform expertise matters, but it only creates value when paired with enterprise-level thinking about people, process, and long-term sustainability.
  • If you're navigating legacy ECM constraints, cloud modernisation, or AI-readiness on top of OpenText — I'm always open to a meaningful conversation.

Philosophy

"I specialize in making enterprise content findable, queryable, and trustworthy at scale."

  • Good architecture is mostly about knowing where to draw boundaries — what to abstract, what to couple, and what to leave explicit. The projects that age well are the ones with clear ownership and boring technology choices.
  • The best migrations I've run are the ones where we verified everything twice and moved fast as a result. The worst ones skipped the preparation and paid for it in production at 2am.
  • Writing forces precision. If I can't explain an architectural decision in plain text, I probably don't understand it well enough. That's why this blog exists.
  • I'm more interested in what RAG gets wrong than what it gets right — hallucination modes, retrieval failures, ACL gaps. Enterprise deployments fail at the edges, not the happy path.
